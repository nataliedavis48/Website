Sam: That liberation is called Moksha. It’s when the soul finally unites with the ultimate reality, which Hindus call Brahman. While there are many gods and goddesses in Hinduism, like Vishnu, Shiva, or Lakshmi, many Hindus believe they are all different forms of this one supreme essence.

Alex: It’s a very diverse system. Now, let’s talk about Buddhism, which actually began in India about two and a half thousand years ago. It started with one man, Siddhartha Gautama.

Sam: Yes, he was a prince who lived a very comfortable life. But when he saw the suffering of the world, he left his palace to find the truth about why people suffer. After years of searching and meditating, he achieved enlightenment under a tree and became the Buddha, which means the Enlightened One.

Alex: The Buddha’s teachings are very practical. He didn't focus much on gods or the creation of the universe. Instead, he taught the Four Noble Truths. The main idea is that life involves suffering, and this suffering is caused by our desires and attachments.

Sam: It sounds a bit sad at first, but the Buddha also gave a solution. He taught the Eightfold Path. This is a guide for how to live a balanced life through right speech, right action, and right meditation. He called this the Middle Way, which means avoiding extremes.

Alex: Just like Hinduism, Buddhism also believes in Karma and the cycle of rebirth. However, the goal in Buddhism is slightly different. It’s called Nirvana. This is a state of peace where there is no more suffering and no more rebirth.

Sam: One of the biggest differences between the two is how they view the social structure. In ancient India, Hinduism was closely tied to the caste system, which divided people into different social levels. The Buddha, however, taught that anyone could achieve enlightenment regardless of their social status.

Alex: That was a very revolutionary idea at the time. It’s probably why Buddhism spread so quickly across Asia. Even though Buddhism eventually became less common in India itself, its principles of non-violence and compassion remained a huge part of Indian identity.
