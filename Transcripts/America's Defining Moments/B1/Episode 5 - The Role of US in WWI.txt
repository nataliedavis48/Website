Sam: Hi Alex! It is great to be here. In our last few episodes, we talked about the early days of the United States. But today, we are moving into the 20th century. We are talking about the First World War, also known as the Great War.

Alex: This is a very interesting topic because, for a long time, the United States did not want to be part of it. The war started in Europe in 1914, but the U.S. stayed neutral for almost three years.

Sam: That is right. Most Americans thought of it as a European conflict. They didn't see why young American men should go across the ocean to fight in a war that didn't involve them. President Woodrow Wilson even won his second election with the slogan, He kept us out of war.

Alex: So, Sam, what changed? Why did the United States finally decide to join the fight in 1917?

Sam: There were a few big reasons. First, Germany was using submarines to sink ships in the Atlantic Ocean. Some of these ships had American passengers. The most famous one was the Lusitania. Even though it was a British ship, many Americans died, and the public was very angry.

Alex: I also remember learning about a secret message, right? The Zimmermann Telegram?

Sam: Exactly. That was the final straw. British intelligence found a secret message from Germany to Mexico. Germany promised to help Mexico take back land from the U.S., like Texas and Arizona, if Mexico joined the war on Germany's side. When Americans found out about this, they were ready to fight.

Alex: When the U.S. finally entered the war in April 1917, it had a huge impact. By that time, the soldiers in France and Britain were completely exhausted. They had been fighting in muddy trenches for years.

Sam: You are right, Alex. The American soldiers were nicknamed Doughboys. When they arrived, they were fresh and full of energy. The U.S. didn't just send men, though. They sent a massive amount of supplies, food, and money. American industry was incredibly powerful.

Alex: It’s amazing how quickly the country changed. Millions of men were drafted into the army, and women started working in factories to help the war effort. This was a big change for American society.

Sam: The war didn't last much longer after the U.S. joined. By November 1918, the fighting stopped. President Wilson wanted to make sure a war like this never happened again. He had a plan called the Fourteen Points.

Alex: He was very focused on creating the League of Nations, wasn't he? He wanted a place where countries could talk about their problems instead of fighting.

Sam: He did. But here is the ironic part. Even though Wilson created the idea, the United States Congress refused to join the League of Nations. Many Americans wanted to go back to a policy of isolationism. They wanted to focus on their own country and stay out of world problems again.

Alex: Even so, the First World War changed America’s place in the world forever. It showed that the United States was now a global power. We couldn't really stay isolated for much longer.

Sam: That is true. It set the stage for everything that happened in the rest of the 20th century.
