Alex: When the stock market crashed, people who had invested all their savings suddenly had nothing. But it wasn't just investors who suffered, right?

Sam: Not at all. Because the banks had invested people's money in the market, many banks ran out of cash and had to close. Imagine going to your bank to get your money, and the doors are locked. The money is simply gone.

Alex: That sounds terrifying. I read that at the height of the Depression, about 25% of Americans were out of work. That is one out of every four people.

Sam: It’s hard to imagine that today. Without jobs, people couldn't pay for their homes. Many families ended up living in "shanty towns," which were groups of small, poor shacks made of wood and cardboard. People actually called them "Hoovervilles" after President Herbert Hoover, because they felt he wasn't doing enough to help.

Alex: And it wasn't just the cities. The farmers in the middle of the country had a terrible time too.

Sam: Oh, definitely. They dealt with the "Dust Bowl." There was a long period without rain, and the soil turned to dust. Huge windstorms literally blew the farms away. Thousands of families had to pack their cars and drive west to California to look for any kind of work.

Alex: It sounds like a very dark time. But things did start to change when a new president was elected, right? Franklin D. Roosevelt?

Sam: Yes, FDR. He was very charismatic. He promised a "New Deal" for the American people. He created all these new government programs to give people jobs. They built roads, bridges, and national parks.

Alex: One of the most famous things he did was start Social Security. That’s the system that still helps retired people in the U.S. today.

Sam: He also used the radio to talk directly to the public. These were called "Fireside Chats." He wanted to give people hope and explain his plans in simple language so they wouldn't feel so afraid.

Alex: So, did the New Deal end the Great Depression?

Sam: It helped a lot, and it gave people their dignity back. However, most historians agree that the economy didn't fully bounce back until World War II started. The country had to build so many planes, tanks, and ships that suddenly everyone had a job again.

Alex: It’s amazing how much that decade shaped the American spirit. People who lived through it became very careful with their money for the rest of their lives.

Sam: My grandmother was like that! She never threw anything away because she remembered what it was like to have nothing. It really was a defining moment for the country.
