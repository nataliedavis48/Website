Sam: Hi Alex! It is great to be here. Today, we are traveling across the world to talk about the education system in China. It is a topic that many people find fascinating because it is so intense and has such a long history.

Alex: You are right, Sam. When we think of education in China today, we often think of high pressure and long hours. But to understand why it is like that, we have to look at the past first.

Sam: China actually had one of the oldest exam systems in the world, didn’t it?

Alex: Yes, it did. It was called the Imperial Examinations, or Keju. It started more than a thousand years ago. Back then, if you wanted to work for the government and have a good life, you had to pass these exams.

Sam: I heard those exams were incredibly difficult. People had to stay in small rooms for days just to write their answers.

Alex: Exactly. They had to memorize thousands of pages of ancient texts and write perfect poems and essays. It was the main way to achieve social mobility. Even if you were born into a poor family, you could become a powerful official if you were smart enough to pass.

Sam: That explains why Chinese culture values education so much today. The idea that a single test can change your life is very old. But things have changed a lot since the ancient times, haven’t they?

Alex: They certainly have. Today, the system is famous for the Gaokao. That is the National College Entrance Exam. It happens every June, and it is a huge event.

Sam: I have seen news reports about the Gaokao. The whole country seems to stop. Construction work pauses so it is quiet, and taxis give free rides to students. It sounds very stressful.

Alex: It is extremely stressful. For many students, those two days of testing decide which university they can attend. Since there is a lot of competition for the best universities, students often study for twelve or fourteen hours a day.

Sam: That is a lot of pressure for a teenager. I remember reading that many students used to go to private tutoring centers after school and even on weekends just to keep up.

Alex: That was a big problem. However, the government recently introduced something called the "Double Reduction" policy. They wanted to reduce the amount of homework and stop for-profit tutoring companies.

Sam: That sounds like a big shift. Why did they do that?

Alex: They wanted to protect the physical and mental health of students. They also wanted to make things more fair. Before the policy, rich families could pay for expensive tutors, which gave their children an unfair advantage. Now, the focus is shifting a bit more toward a balanced life.

Sam: It is interesting to see how they are trying to modernize while still keeping that traditional respect for hard work. Is the curriculum changing too?

Alex: Yes, there is a much bigger focus on science, technology, and innovation now. China wants to be a global leader in those areas, so schools are investing heavily in STEM subjects.

Sam: It sounds like the system is in a period of big changes. From memorizing ancient poems to building robots and focusing on mental health.

Alex: Exactly. It is a mix of deep tradition and a fast-moving future. Well, that is all the time we have for today.
