Alex: Hi everyone, and welcome to another episode of our travel and culture podcast. I’m Alex, and today I’m joined by Sam. We’re heading to the Caribbean, but perhaps not to one of the islands you might first think of.

Sam: That’s right, Alex. We are talking about Curaçao. It’s a small, stunning island located just off the coast of South America. Lately, it’s been making headlines for something other than its beautiful beaches—its soccer team!

Alex: It really is impressive. For such a small population, they are doing incredibly well in the World Cup qualifiers. Many people are asking: how can an island with fewer than 160,000 people be so competitive on the global stage?

Sam: It’s a fascinating story. A lot of it comes down to the "Dutch connection." Because Curaçao is part of the Kingdom of the Netherlands, many players have dual nationality. They might be born or trained in the Netherlands, playing in top European leagues, but they choose to represent their Caribbean heritage. When you combine European tactical training with Caribbean passion, you get a very dangerous team.

Alex: They’ve also had some very famous international coaches who have professionalized the national team. It’s a great example of how a small nation can punch above its weight. But Sam, if we visited Curaçao to watch a game, what language would we hear the fans speaking in the stands?

Sam: You’d hear a real mix! The official languages are Dutch, English, and Papiamentu. Papiamentu is the one that really defines the island. It’s a beautiful, melodic language that blends Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch, and even some African and Hebrew influences. Most locals are multilingual, so you’ll have no trouble communicating in English or Spanish either.

Alex: That sounds like a linguist's dream. And the capital city, Willemstad, looks like a dream too. I’ve seen those famous photos of the bright, colorful houses along the water.

Sam: Willemstad is actually a UNESCO World Heritage site. The architecture looks exactly like Amsterdam, but painted in vibrant pinks, yellows, and blues. The history there is quite deep. The island was originally inhabited by the Arawak people, then colonized by the Spanish, and later the Dutch in the 17th century. It became a major center for trade.

Alex: Speaking of history, I was surprised to learn about the Jewish community there. It’s one of the oldest in the Western Hemisphere, isn't it?

Sam: It is. Jewish settlers arrived in the 1650s, fleeing the Inquisition in Europe and Brazil. They founded the Mikvé Israel-Emanuel Synagogue in Willemstad, which is the oldest synagogue in continuous use in the Americas. A unique feature is its floor, which is covered in white sand.

Alex: Why sand?

Sam: It’s a tradition to remind the congregation of the 40 years the Israelites spent wandering the desert, and also to honor their ancestors who had to muffle the sound of their footsteps while praying in secret during times of persecution.

Alex: That is such a moving detail. It really shows that Curaçao is much more than just a place for sunbathing. But, speaking of sun, is it actually an interesting place for a typical tourist to visit?

Sam: Absolutely. Unlike many other Caribbean islands, Curaçao is located outside the main hurricane belt, so the weather is almost always sunny and dry. The diving and snorkeling are world-class because the coral reefs are so close to the shore. Plus, the culture is a true melting pot. You can enjoy Dutch cheese, Caribbean seafood, and vibrant nightlife all in one day.

Alex: It sounds like the perfect mix of sport, history, and relaxation. I think I’m ready to book my flight and cheer for their soccer team in person!

Sam: I’ll join you! It’s definitely a hidden gem that deserves more attention.
