However, the way Rwanda has handled the aftermath is what makes it so unique. They have focused heavily on national unity and reconciliation rather than revenge.

Alex: That leads us to the question of its current status. Is Rwanda a rich or a poor country?

Sam: It is still classified as a developing nation, so it isn't rich in the way Western countries are. Most people still work in agriculture. However, it has one of the fastest-growing economies on the continent. They are trying to become a high-tech hub for Africa. They have invested a lot in fiber-optic internet and infrastructure.

Alex: What about the political side? Is it a democracy or a dictatorship?

Sam: That is a bit of a gray area and a topic of much debate. On one hand, you have President Paul Kagame, who has been in power for over two decades. His supporters say he brought stability, safety, and economic growth after the war. On the other hand, some human rights groups and Western critics argue that there is very little room for political opposition or freedom of speech. So, while there are elections, many describe it as an authoritarian state that delivers high efficiency.

Alex: It sounds like a complicated balance. How do they get along with their neighbors and the West?

Sam: Their relationship with the West, especially the US and the UK, is generally very strong. They are seen as a reliable partner for development and peacekeeping missions. However, relations with their neighbor, the Democratic Republic of Congo, are often very tense. There are frequent accusations regarding border security and support for rebel groups, which makes the region a bit unstable at times.

Alex: With all that complexity, the big question for our listeners is: is it worth visiting? And more importantly, is it safe for tourists?

The biggest draw is definitely the mountain gorillas. It is one of the few places in the world where you can see them in the wild. It is expensive, but it is a bucket-list experience for many.
