Alex: Welcome to today’s episode of World Wonders. I’m Alex, and today we are diving into a topic that often confuses people when they look at a map of East Africa. We’re talking about Somaliland.

Sam: It is a fascinating place, Alex. Many people hear the name and immediately think of Somalia, but the reality on the ground is quite different. To answer the most common question first: Is it part of Somalia? Well, it depends on who you ask.

Alex: That is a tricky start! Internationally, most countries and the United Nations still consider it part of Somalia. However, Somaliland has been acting as a fully independent state for over thirty years. It has its own government, its own currency, and even its own passport.

Sam: Exactly. To understand why, we have to look at when it was established. Somaliland actually has a very specific history. It was a British protectorate until June 1960. For five days in 1960, it was an independent country before it decided to join with the former Italian Somalia to form the Somali Republic.

Alex: But that union didn’t last, did it?

Sam: No, it didn't. After years of tension and a brutal civil war in the 1980s, Somaliland decided to break away and reaffirmed its independence in May 1991. While the rest of Somalia struggled with instability, Somaliland began to rebuild itself.

Alex: For those who aren't sure where it is, could you describe its location?

Sam: It’s located in the Horn of Africa. It sits on the southern coast of the Gulf of Aden. It shares borders with Djibouti to the northwest, Ethiopia to the south and west, and Somalia to the east. Its location is actually very strategic for global trade.

Alex: Speaking of the people living there, what language would I need to know if I visited?

Sam: The primary language is Somali, which is beautiful and very poetic. However, because of their history and religion, Arabic is also very common. You’ll find that many people in the cities speak English quite well too, especially since it is used in higher education.

Alex: You mentioned the government earlier. Is Somaliland a democracy or a dictatorship?

Sam: This is one of the most impressive things about it. Despite not being recognized by the world, Somaliland is a functioning democracy. They have held multiple presidential and parliamentary elections with peaceful transfers of power. It’s often called a "miracle" of stability in a region that has seen a lot of conflict.

Alex: That sounds very positive. But what about the economy? Is it a rich or poor country?

Sam: By global standards, it is still considered a poor, developing region. They face high unemployment and a lack of international investment because of their legal status. Their economy relies heavily on livestock—exporting millions of sheep and goats to Arab countries, especially during the Hajj season. However, they also get a lot of money from the "diaspora," which are Somalilanders living abroad who send money back home.

Alex: And what about their relations with other countries? If they aren't officially recognized, how do they talk to the West or their neighbors?

Sam: It’s a bit of a "don’t ask, don’t tell" situation. Ethiopia is a very close partner because they use Somaliland’s Berbera port for trade. The West, including the UK and the US, works closely with Somaliland on security and development, even if they don't give them a seat at the UN. Arab countries, like the UAE, have also invested heavily in their infrastructure lately.

Alex: We should mention religion as well. I assume it plays a big role in daily life?

Sam: Absolutely. Almost 100% of the population is Muslim, and Islam is the state religion. It’s a conservative society, so visitors should be respectful of local customs, especially regarding dress and alcohol, which is prohibited.

Alex: Now, the big question for our listeners who love to travel: Is it safe? And is it actually worth visiting?

Sam: Surprisingly, yes! Compared to many of its neighbors, Somaliland is very safe for foreigners. Violent crime is rare. As for whether it’s worth visiting, if you are an adventurous traveler, definitely. The capital, Hargeisa, has a vibrant market culture. But the real highlight is Laas Geel.

Alex: I’ve seen photos of that! Those are the ancient cave paintings, right?

Sam: Yes! They are some of the oldest and best-preserved rock art in Africa, dating back over five thousand years. Because there aren't many tourists, you can often stand in those caves all by yourself. It’s a powerful experience.

Alex: It sounds like Somaliland is a place that defies expectations. It’s a democracy that officially doesn’t exist, a safe haven in a tough neighborhood, and a place with a very deep history.

Sam: Well said, Alex. It’s definitely a place the world should pay more attention to.
