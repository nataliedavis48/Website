Alex: That is a great topic, Sam. Many people see the name and think it is just another part of Somalia. But if you actually go there, the reality is very different.

Sam: Exactly. Let’s start with the most important question. Is Somaliland part of Somalia or is it an independent state?

Alex: Well, it depends on who you ask. If you ask the United Nations or any other country in the world, they will tell you it is legally part of Somalia. However, in reality, Somaliland has been acting as a completely independent state since 1991. They have their own flag, their own money, their own army, and their own elected president. In the political world, we call this a "de facto" independent state.

Sam: So, they broke away from Somalia over thirty years ago? What led to that?

Alex: It is a long story, but to keep it simple, Somaliland was actually a British protectorate before 1960. The rest of Somalia was controlled by Italy. When they both got independence in 1960, they decided to join together to form one big country. But the union didn't work well. After a very long and painful civil war in the 1980s, Somaliland decided to break away and reclaim its independence in 1991.

Sam: That is fascinating. Since they are on their own now, what is the government like? Is it a democracy or a dictatorship?

Alex: This is where Somaliland really stands out. While many countries in that part of the world have struggled with dictators or constant fighting, Somaliland is a stable democracy. They have held many elections where the people vote for their leaders. They even have a system where traditional elders and modern politicians work together. It is often called a "hidden democracy" because the world doesn't talk about it much.

Sam: That sounds very positive. Where is the center of all this activity? What is the capital city?

Alex: The capital is Hargeisa. It is a very busy, growing city with more than a million people. It doesn't have many skyscrapers like Dubai or New York, but it is full of markets, colorful shops, and very friendly people.

Sam: And what about the culture? What language do they speak and what is the main religion?

Alex: Almost everyone speaks Somali. It is a beautiful, poetic language. Because of their history and religion, many people also speak Arabic. English is quite common too, especially among young people and in business. Regarding religion, almost 100 percent of the population is Sunni Muslim. Islam is the foundation of their culture and laws.

Sam: Let’s talk about the economy. Is Somaliland a rich country?

Alex: To be honest, it is still a poor, developing country. Because they are not officially recognized by the UN, they cannot get loans from the World Bank or big international organizations. Most of their money comes from selling livestock, like goats, sheep, and camels, to Arab countries like Saudi Arabia. They also rely on remittances, which is money sent home by Somalilanders living in Europe or North America.

Sam: You mentioned Arab countries. How are their relationships with their neighbors and the West?

Alex: They have a very strong relationship with Ethiopia because Ethiopia is a landlocked country and needs to use Somaliland’s ports. They also have a big deal with a company from the United Arab Emirates to develop the port of Berbera. The West, like the US and the UK, works with them on security and fighting pirates, but they haven't given them official recognition yet. They are worried that recognizing Somaliland might cause more trouble in the rest of Somalia.

Sam: That sounds like a difficult diplomatic situation. Now, for the travelers listening—is it safe for tourists? Is it worth visiting?

Alex: This is the most surprising part for many people. While Somalia is often dangerous, Somaliland is very safe for foreigners. You can walk through the streets of Hargeisa during the day without feeling scared. The people are incredibly welcoming. If you sit in a tea shop, someone will likely come over to say "Welcome!" and ask you about your home.

Sam: And what should a tourist see there?

Alex: The number one spot is Laas Geel. It is a site with cave paintings that are over 5,000 years old. They are some of the best-preserved ancient art in all of Africa. The colors are still bright and beautiful. There are also great beaches in the city of Berbera, though they are very quiet and not developed for tourism yet.

Sam: It sounds like a hidden gem for someone who wants an adventure. It’s a place with a complicated legal status but a very clear identity and a peaceful life.

Alex: Exactly. It is a place that is proud of its peace and is waiting for the rest of the world to notice.
