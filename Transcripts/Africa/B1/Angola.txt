Alex: I’m really excited about this one, Sam. Angola is a huge country, but I feel like many people don’t know much about it. For starters, I’ve always wondered about the language. Why is Portuguese the official language there?

Sam: That’s a great question to begin with. The reason is history. Angola was a colony of Portugal for a very long time—about four hundred years, actually. It only gained its independence in 1975. Because of those centuries of colonial rule, Portuguese became the language of government, education, and daily life in the cities. 

Alex: Do people speak other languages too?

Sam: Absolutely. There are dozens of indigenous languages like Umbundu and Kimbundu. However, Portuguese is what connects all the different ethnic groups. It’s also why Angolan culture, especially the music and food, has a unique mix of African and Portuguese influences.

Alex: That sounds like a beautiful mix. But I know the path after independence wasn't easy. Didn't they have a very long conflict?

Sam: Yes, they did. Almost immediately after becoming independent in 1975, a terrible civil war broke out. It lasted for twenty-seven years and didn't end until 2002. This war really shaped the country we see today. It destroyed a lot of the buildings and roads, and it forced many people to move to the capital city, Luanda, to find safety.

Alex: That explains why the country is still developing in some areas. What about the government now? Is Angola a democracy or a dictatorship?

Sam: It’s a bit of a transition. On paper, Angola is a multi-party democracy. They hold elections and have a parliament. However, the same political party, the MPLA, has been in power since 1975. For decades, it was seen as a very authoritarian system under their former president. But since 2017, the new president, João Lourenço, has been making efforts to change things. He is trying to fight corruption and give the people more freedom, though many people say there is still a long way to go.

Alex: It’s interesting to see a country trying to change its political identity. Now, let’s talk about money and life. How is the standard of living there compared to other African countries?

Sam: It’s a country of big contrasts. Angola is actually very rich because it has massive amounts of oil and diamonds. In fact, it’s one of the largest oil producers in Africa. This makes its economy quite strong compared to its neighbors like Zambia or the Democratic Republic of the Congo. However, the wealth isn't shared equally. 

Alex: I’ve heard that Luanda is incredibly expensive.

Sam: It really is! For several years, Luanda was ranked as the most expensive city in the world for foreigners. Because so many things have to be imported, prices for milk, rent, or cars are very high. So, while some people in the city are very wealthy, many people in the countryside still live in poverty. The infrastructure, like schools and hospitals, is still catching up after the years of war.

Alex: That’s a difficult balance to find. What about their relations with the West? Are they friendly with countries like the U.S. or European nations?

Sam: Yes, very much so. During the Cold War, Angola was closer to the Soviet Union, but that has changed. Today, Angola is a key partner for the United States and Europe, mainly because of energy. They want Western companies to invest in their oil and also help them diversify their economy so they don't just rely on one resource. They are also working closely with China on big construction projects.

Alex: It sounds like they are very active on the global stage. So, Sam, if I wanted to visit, is it safe for tourists?

Sam: Generally, yes, but you have to be smart. Most of the country is safe, and the people are incredibly welcoming. The nature is breathtaking—they have the Kalandula Falls, which are some of the largest waterfalls in Africa. However, in Luanda, you have to be careful about street crime, just like in any other major city. You shouldn't show off expensive phones or jewelry. But if you have a guide and stay in the right areas, it’s a wonderful place to explore.

Sam: That’s a perfect way to put it. It’s a resilient nation. It’s moving away from its history of war and trying to build a modern, stable society. It’s definitely a place to watch in the coming years.
