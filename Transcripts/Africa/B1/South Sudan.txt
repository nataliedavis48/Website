Sam: That is a tough question to answer simply. On paper, it is a republic with a president. However, since 2013, the country has struggled with a lot of internal conflict and civil wars between different political groups. Because of this, many people describe the government as authoritarian or a "transitional" government that hasn't quite reached a stable democracy yet. There haven't been regular, free elections like you might see in other countries.

Alex: That sounds very unstable for the people living there. Is the country poor or rich?

Sam: This is the big tragedy of South Sudan. In terms of natural resources, it is actually very rich. It has a lot of oil, and the land is very fertile because of the Nile River. It could be an agricultural powerhouse. But because of the constant fighting, the economy is in a terrible state. It is currently one of the poorest countries in the world. Most people live on less than two dollars a day and struggle to find enough food or clean water.

Alex: It’s heartbreaking when a country has so much potential but can't use it because of war. How do they get along with their other neighbors, like Kenya or Uganda?

Sam: Generally, South Sudan has close ties with its neighbors. Many South Sudanese people lived as refugees in Kenya and Uganda during the wars, so there are strong cultural and family links. South Sudan is also a member of the East African Community. However, there are sometimes tensions over refugees and border security.

Alex: And what about the West? How are their relations with countries like the United States or members of the European Union?

Sam: The West was very supportive of South Sudan’s independence in 2011. Since then, the US and Europe have provided billions of dollars in humanitarian aid. However, the relationship has become strained. The West has put sanctions on some South Sudanese leaders because of human rights concerns and the failure to stop the internal fighting.
