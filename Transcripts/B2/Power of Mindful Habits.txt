Hello and welcome. My name is Lauren. Today, we're going to talk about something that affects all of us: our daily habits. We often think about habits for physical health, like going to the gym. But what about habits for our mental health? We'll explore how small, daily actions can shape our lives for the better. Our discussion today is inspired by the work of Dr. Seth Gillihan, an expert in mental wellness.
Think about it. We know that if we exercise consistently, our bodies get stronger. It’s the same for our minds. Research shows that simple daily habits, like talking with a friend, walking in a park, or doing an activity you enjoy, can lead to better mental health. Even the way we talk to ourselves matters. Practicing positive self-talk, or affirmations, can improve our self-esteem and help us feel better overall.
Dr. Gillihan has seen how powerful these mental habits are. He wrote a book called 'Your Daily Reset' to help people build them. He says the biggest challenge for many of us is making new, positive ways of thinking a regular part of our daily lives. The method he uses is called Mindful Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, or CBT for short.
So, what is Mindful CBT? Dr. Gillihan explains it has three main parts. Let’s break them down. First is Mindfulness. This is about being in the present moment, right here, right now. It helps us focus on what we can control today, instead of worrying too much about the future.
For example, you can practice mindfulness by simply paying attention to your breath for a few minutes. Feel the sensation of the air entering your nostrils and filling your lungs. This small act can anchor you in the present and create a sense of calm.
Second is the Cognitive part. 'Cognitive' is related to our thoughts. This part helps us see when our mind is telling us things that aren't true—like the lies our mind sometimes tells us. For example, your mind might say, 'No one will talk to me at the party.' CBT helps you question that negative thought and reduce the stress it causes.
The third part is Behavior. This part is about action. It gives you practical things to do to reduce stress. For instance, if you have a very big project that feels overwhelming, CBT helps you make a plan to do it one small step at a time. This makes the big task feel much more manageable.
These three parts—Mindfulness, Cognitive, and Behavior—work together as a team. Mindfulness allows you to notice your thoughts and feelings without judgment. The Cognitive part helps you challenge the negative thoughts you've become aware of. Finally, the Behavior part provides you with a positive action to take, breaking the negative cycle. For instance, if you feel anxious about a social event, mindfulness helps you recognize that feeling. The cognitive part helps you question if your social fears are accurate. And the behavior part encourages you to attend the event, even if just for a little while, to prove to yourself that you can manage the situation.
Dr. Gillihan says that without intentional practice, our minds often fall into unhelpful patterns, like criticizing ourselves. A small, daily reminder can help break these negative cycles. When you make these small changes, or 'resets,' a daily habit, you start to guide your life in a positive direction. Even a simple ritual like reading a book for a few minutes each day can be powerful. It sends a message to yourself that you are important and worth taking care of.
The main message from Dr. Gillihan is that managing stress doesn't have to be a huge, difficult job. Brief, daily practices can bring real relief, and they can fit into even the busiest schedule. He also wants everyone to remember that you are probably doing much better than you think. And if you are struggling, it doesn't mean something is wrong with you—it's a normal part of life. With small, consistent habits, you can build a healthier, happier mind. Thank you for listening today.
